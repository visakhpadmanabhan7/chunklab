The Lighthouse at Vinga

For forty years the lamp at Vinga had not gone dark, and Elsa Lindqvist intended
to keep it that way. She had inherited the post from her father, who had taken it
from his mother, and the rhythm of the light was as much a part of her body as
her own pulse. Three flashes, then darkness; three flashes, then darkness. On
clear nights she could read by the spill of it against the whitewashed wall.

The storm came up from the southwest on the night of the equinox. Elsa had seen
the barometer falling all afternoon, the needle sliding down with a patience that
frightened her more than any sudden drop. By dusk the sea had turned the colour
of pewter, and the gulls had gone quiet and inland, which was always the surest
sign. She filled the lamp's reservoir, trimmed the wick twice, and carried an
extra can of oil up the spiral stair, counting the steps out of old habit.

Near midnight the wind found its full voice. It came against the tower in long
shoulders, and the glass hummed in its frame. Elsa kept her station, turning the
clockwork that drove the lens, and watched the beam sweep out over water she
could no longer see. Somewhere beyond the reef a ship was trying to make the
channel; she had caught the brief green of its starboard lamp between squalls.

She thought of her father then, of how he had told her that the keeper's only
real enemy was sleep, and that a light untended was worse than no light at all,
for it taught sailors to trust what could fail them. So she did not sleep. She
walked the gallery in the screaming dark, wiped the salt from the glass, and fed
the flame, and when at last the grey morning came and the wind fell away, the
ship lay safe at anchor in the lee of the island, its crew lining the rail to
look up at the small bright window where she stood.

They never spoke, the sailors and the keeper. There was too much water between
them, and the language of the light had already said everything that mattered.
Elsa went down at last to sleep, and the lamp, as it had for forty years, waited
patiently for the dark.
